How to read this

The orange line is empirical — it's the cheapest frontier-tier model available on each date, scored at the workload mix you picked. The orange dashed line is the regression's forward projection. The green dashed line is the assumption that matters most: at what per-token cost does serving inference actually break even?

If your cost-to-serve line is ABOVE the orange line
The market is in active subsidy.

Providers are pricing below their fully-loaded cost. This is the regime today by SemiAnalysis's mid estimate. Either (a) the cost estimate is too high, (b) somebody upstream (hyperscaler, VC, government) is eating the loss, or (c) prices are about to move. Plan for (c) eventually.

If your cost-to-serve line is BELOW the orange line
The market is healthy at current prices.

Margins are real, the floor reflects supply-demand equilibrium, and the multi-year decline was supply-side wins (better GPUs, better algorithms) rather than subsidy. In this world, further price drops are still possible but won't be subsidy-driven.

If your forecast line crosses the cost-to-serve line during the horizon
That's the moment the regime breaks.

Either prices stop falling (subsidy gives way to cost-floor) or cost-to-serve falls to meet them (capacity catches up). The Subsidy Runway tile estimates this crossover date from the regression slope and your assumption.

Methodology

The projection is a least-squares linear fit of log($/task) against time, computed on the last twelve months of observations. Linear-in-log gives multiplicative decay (which fits historical data better than linear). The 1-σ band is derived from the regression residuals. The scenario overlays are layered on top — they don't replace the central projection but show alternative paths.

The forecast does not account for: known upcoming model launches, capacity announcements, regulatory shifts, or any qualitative factor a human would weigh. It's pattern continuation. Use it as one input, not the whole picture.
